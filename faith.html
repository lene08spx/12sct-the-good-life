How have early Greek philosohpers shaped a Catholic understanding of a good life?

Socrates: The goodness of a life is determined if you can save yourself and avoid complacency. (Need to be critical of oneself, and question why you do things.)
Aristotle: The goodness of a life is determined by the level of self-improvement, virtuosity, thoughtfulness, and self-awareness.

What did early Christian writers say about a good life?

Adhering to practices, virtues, beliefs.
Thomas Aquinas: A life of preparing for heaven. We can only live in imperfect happiness. A good life is doing just things (service, listening, respectfulness) which bring us closer to God.

What does celebrating the sacraments in the Catholic Church have to do with leading a good life?

Living by principles, 'self-restraint', service. Sacraments guide us to live by Christian values through our relationship with God.

Good Life

Faith is something that has guided decisions, provided justification, and change the course of history. The Ancient Greek philosophers Socrates and Aristotle both saw a good life as being able to judge and reflect upon oneself. These writing however were influenced by the early Christian faith. Thomas Aquinas constantly extolled the idea that the perfect life exists only in heaven. He argued that we could never achieve perfect happiness, but should strive for it through service, respect and by listening to others genuinely. The catholic faith, and by extension the vast majority of Christian traditions, view the sacraments as a way of leading a good life. This relates to the Jewish idea of ritual adherence, but can be better justified with the idea of service and self-restraint. Having a relationship with God allows us to have more meaningful relationships with others.
